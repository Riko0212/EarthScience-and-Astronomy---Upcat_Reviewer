Earth Science

Rocks and Minerals

What is a Mineral?

A mineral is a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and a crystalline structure. All minerals must satisfy five criteria: naturally occurring, inorganic (not made by living organisms), solid, definite chemical composition, and crystalline atomic structure.

Key Properties Used to Identify Minerals: Color, Luster (metallic vs. non-metallic), Hardness (Mohs scale), Cleavage or Fracture, Streak (color of powder), Specific Gravity, Crystal Form, and Special Properties (magnetism, fluorescence, taste).

The Three Rock Types

🔥 Igneous Rock Formed from cooled magma or lava. Intrusive (slow cooling, large crystals): granite. Extrusive (fast cooling, small or no crystals): basalt, obsidian, pumice.
📚 Sedimentary Rock Formed from compacted and cemented sediments. Clastic: sandstone, shale. Chemical: limestone, rock salt. Organic: coal, chalk. Contains fossils.
♨️ Metamorphic Rock Formed when existing rocks are changed by heat and/or pressure. Foliated (layered): slate, schist, gneiss. Non-foliated: marble (from limestone), quartzite (from sandstone).

The Rock Cycle

The rock cycle is the continuous process by which rocks are created, changed, and destroyed. No rock type is permanent — all rocks can be transformed into another type given enough time and the right conditions.

1
Magma cools → Igneous rock forms
2
Weathering & erosion break down igneous rock → sediments
3
Compaction & cementation of sediments → Sedimentary rock
4
Heat and pressure on any rock → Metamorphic rock
5
Melting of any rock → Magma (cycle repeats)

Earth's Layers

CrustOutermost layer; 5–70 km thick. Oceanic crust = basalt (denser). Continental crust = granite (less dense).
Mantle~2,900 km thick. Mostly solid silicate rock, but the upper part (asthenosphere) flows slowly like putty. Source of magma.
Outer CoreLiquid iron and nickel. Generates Earth's magnetic field through convection currents.
Inner CoreSolid iron and nickel. ~1,200 km radius. Despite extreme heat, immense pressure keeps it solid.
⭐ Did You Know? Earth's inner core temperature reaches approximately 5,000–6,000°C — similar to the surface of the Sun!

Fossils

A fossil is the preserved remains or trace of an organism that lived in the past. Fossils are typically found in sedimentary rock because these rocks form through the gradual layering of sediments, which can trap and preserve organisms.

Types of Fossils

Body FossilsActual remains: bones, shells, teeth, or soft tissue (rare). Most common type.
Trace FossilsEvidence of activity: footprints, burrows, feeding marks, coprolites (fossilized dung).
Mold & CastMold = impression left in rock. Cast = mold filled with minerals forming a 3D replica.
Petrified FossilsOriginal material replaced by minerals. Example: petrified wood, where silica replaces wood cells.
Carbon FilmThin carbon residue left after organic matter decays. Preserves leaves, fish, and soft tissue outlines.
Preserved in AmberOrganisms trapped in tree resin that hardens. Insects, small animals perfectly preserved.

How Fossilization Occurs

1
Death — Organism dies, preferably near water
2
Quick burial — Covered by sediment before scavengers or decomposition destroys it
3
Permineralization — Minerals in groundwater fill pores in bones and shells
4
Compaction — Sediment layers build up, turning to rock over millions of years
5
Exposure — Erosion or geological activity brings fossils to the surface

Fossil Record & Geologic Time

The fossil record is the collection of all known fossils arranged in the order of geological time. It gives us a history of life on Earth and is organized by the Geologic Time Scale — eons, eras, periods, and epochs. Older fossils are found in deeper rock layers (the Law of Superposition). Index fossils are fossils of organisms that lived for a short time but were widespread — they help scientists date rock layers precisely.

Law of Superposition: In undisturbed rock layers, the oldest layers are at the bottom and the youngest are at the top. This principle is fundamental to relative age dating.

Radioactive Dating

Absolute dating uses the decay rates of radioactive isotopes (half-lives) to determine the actual age of fossils and rocks. Carbon-14 is used for materials up to ~50,000 years old. Uranium-238 and Potassium-40 are used for much older materials (millions to billions of years).

Plate Tectonics

The theory of plate tectonics states that Earth's lithosphere (crust + upper mantle) is broken into large pieces called tectonic plates that float on the semi-fluid asthenosphere and move slowly over time. This theory unified many geological observations including continental drift, seafloor spreading, earthquakes, and volcanoes.

Evidence for Continental Drift (Alfred Wegener, 1912)

  • The continents fit together like puzzle pieces (especially South America and Africa)
  • Same fossils found on different continents separated by ocean (e.g., Glossopteris fern)
  • Matching rock formations and mountain ranges across ocean basins
  • Evidence of ancient glaciation in tropical regions (Gondwana)

Seafloor Spreading

Harry Hess (1960s) proposed that new oceanic crust is created at mid-ocean ridges where magma rises, spreads, and solidifies. This process pushes older crust away. Evidence: magnetic stripes in seafloor rocks showing alternating magnetic polarity, youngest rocks at ridges, oldest at trenches.

Earthquakes

Earthquakes occur when stress built up along faults is suddenly released as seismic waves. The focus (hypocenter) is the underground point where the earthquake originates. The epicenter is the point on the surface directly above the focus.

P-Waves (Primary)Compressional waves, travel through solids, liquids, and gases. First to arrive. Push-pull motion.
S-Waves (Secondary)Shear waves, travel through solids only. Arrive after P-waves. Side-to-side motion.
Surface WavesTravel along Earth's surface. Slowest but cause most damage. Two types: Love waves and Rayleigh waves.
Richter ScaleLogarithmic scale measuring earthquake magnitude. Each whole number = 10× amplitude increase, ~31.6× energy release.

Volcanoes

Volcanoes form where magma reaches the surface. Found at plate boundaries and over hot spots (e.g., Hawaii). Types:

Shield VolcanoBroad, gently sloping. Low-viscosity basaltic lava. Quiet eruptions. Example: Mauna Loa, Hawaii.
Composite/StratovolcanoSteep, layered. High-viscosity, explosive eruptions. Example: Mt. Fuji, Mt. St. Helens.
Cinder ConeSmallest, steep. Made of volcanic cinders. Short, explosive eruptions. Example: Paricutin, Mexico.
⭐ The Ring of Fire is a horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean where ~90% of the world's earthquakes and ~75% of all volcanoes are found — all due to active plate boundaries.

Air, Weather, and Climate

Earth's Atmosphere

The atmosphere is a thin layer of gases surrounding Earth held in place by gravity. It protects life by absorbing UV radiation, regulating temperature, and enabling weather.

LayerAltitudeKey Features
Troposphere0–12 kmWhere weather occurs; temperature decreases with altitude; contains 75–80% of atmosphere's mass
Stratosphere12–50 kmContains ozone layer (absorbs UV); temperature increases with altitude; where jets fly
Mesosphere50–80 kmColdest layer (−90°C); meteors burn up here
Thermosphere80–700 kmVery high temps (up to 2,000°C); auroras occur here; ISS orbits here
Exosphere700+ kmOutermost; merges with space; satellites orbit here

Composition of Air

Dry air is approximately: 78% Nitrogen (N₂), 21% Oxygen (O₂), 0.9% Argon (Ar), 0.04% Carbon Dioxide (CO₂), plus trace gases.

Weather vs. Climate

WeatherShort-term atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time (temperature, humidity, precipitation, wind). Changes hour to hour.
ClimateAverage weather patterns of a region over a long period (30+ years). Determined by latitude, altitude, proximity to water, ocean currents.

The Water Cycle (Hydrological Cycle)

1
Evaporation & Transpiration — Water from oceans, lakes, and plants enters the atmosphere as vapor
2
Condensation — Water vapor cools and forms clouds (liquid droplets or ice crystals)
3
Precipitation — Water falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
4
Collection & Runoff — Water collects in oceans, rivers, groundwater; cycle repeats

Types of Clouds

CumulusPuffy, flat-bottomed. Fair weather. If they grow tall → Cumulonimbus (thunderstorms).
StratusFlat, layered, gray. Cover sky like a blanket. Drizzle or light rain. Low altitude.
CirrusWispy, feathery, high altitude. Made of ice crystals. Indicate fair weather or approaching front.
NimbostratusDark, thick stratus clouds producing continuous rain or snow. Cover entire sky.

Air Masses and Fronts

An air mass is a large body of air with uniform temperature and humidity. When two different air masses meet, a front forms:

  • Cold Front: Cold air pushes under warm air → sudden storms, then cooler weather
  • Warm Front: Warm air slides over cold air → gradual cloud thickening, light rain
  • Stationary Front: Two air masses stall → prolonged clouds and precipitation
  • Occluded Front: Cold front catches warm front → complex weather

The Greenhouse Effect & Climate Change

The greenhouse effect is a natural process where greenhouse gases (CO₂, methane, water vapor, nitrous oxide) trap heat from the Sun in Earth's atmosphere, keeping the planet warm enough to support life (~15°C average). Human activities increase CO₂ and methane, enhancing this effect and causing global warming.

Effects of Climate Change: Rising sea levels, melting glaciers and ice caps, more frequent extreme weather events, shifts in ecosystems and habitats, ocean acidification from absorbed CO₂, and disruption of agricultural patterns.

Water on Earth

About 71% of Earth's surface is covered by water. Of all water on Earth: ~97.5% is saltwater (oceans), ~2.5% is freshwater. Of that freshwater, ~69% is frozen in glaciers and ice caps, ~30% is groundwater, and less than 1% is surface freshwater (rivers, lakes).

The Hydrosphere

The hydrosphere includes all water on Earth: oceans, rivers, lakes, groundwater, glaciers, ice sheets, and water in the atmosphere. It constantly interacts with the lithosphere, atmosphere, and biosphere.

Ocean Features

Continental ShelfShallow, gradually sloping extension of continents under the ocean. Rich in marine life and resources.
Continental SlopeSteeper drop-off at the edge of the continental shelf. Leads to the deep ocean floor.
Abyssal PlainFlat, deep ocean floor. 4,000–6,000 m deep. Coldest, darkest part of ocean.
Ocean TrenchesDeepest parts of ocean. Formed by subduction. Mariana Trench: ~11,000 m — deepest point on Earth.
Mid-Ocean RidgeUnderwater mountain range where seafloor spreading occurs. Longest mountain range on Earth.

Ocean Currents

Surface currents are driven by wind patterns and Earth's rotation (Coriolis effect). They form large circular patterns called gyres. They significantly affect regional climates — warm currents warm coastal areas; cold currents cool them.

Deep ocean currents (thermohaline circulation) are driven by differences in water density due to temperature and salinity. Known as the "Global Ocean Conveyor Belt," this slow circulation distributes heat and nutrients throughout the world's oceans.

Groundwater & Watersheds

Groundwater fills pores in soil and rock below the water table. An aquifer is a layer of permeable rock that stores and transmits groundwater. A watershed (drainage basin) is an area of land that drains into a common body of water. Proper watershed management is critical for freshwater supply.

⭐ Did You Know? The world's largest aquifer is the Ogallala Aquifer in the United States, stretching under 8 states and providing water to ~30% of all groundwater used for irrigation in the US.

Erosion and Deposition by Water

Moving water is the primary agent of erosion on Earth's surface. Rivers carve valleys, gorges, and canyons. Water deposits sediment to form deltas, alluvial fans, floodplains, and sandbars. The Grand Canyon was carved by the Colorado River over ~5–6 million years.

Energy Resources

Non-Renewable Resources

Non-renewable resources are formed over millions of years and cannot be replaced on a human timescale. Once used, they are gone.

CoalFormed from ancient plant material. Burned for electricity and heat. Most carbon-intensive fuel. Produces CO₂, SO₂, and ash.
Petroleum (Oil)Formed from ancient marine organisms. Used for gasoline, diesel, plastics. Extracted by drilling. Transported via pipelines and tankers.
Natural GasPrimarily methane (CH₄). Cleaner-burning than coal and oil. Extracted via drilling; used for heating and electricity.
Nuclear EnergyFission of uranium-235 or plutonium-239 releases enormous heat to generate electricity. No CO₂ emissions; radioactive waste is a concern.

Renewable Resources

☀️ SolarPhotovoltaic cells convert sunlight to electricity. Solar thermal systems use heat. Abundant, clean. Depends on sunlight availability.
💨 WindWind turbines convert kinetic energy of wind to electricity. Clean, renewable. Best in open plains and offshore. Depends on consistent wind.
💧 HydroelectricFlowing water drives turbines. Large dams and run-of-river systems. Clean but disrupts river ecosystems and fish migration.
🌿 BiomassBurning organic material (wood, crops, waste). Can be carbon-neutral if sustainably managed. Also produces biofuels (ethanol, biodiesel).
🌋 GeothermalHeat from Earth's interior used for heating and electricity. Especially viable in volcanic regions (Iceland, Philippines).
🌊 Tidal/WaveOcean tides and waves drive generators. Predictable but limited by geography. Still largely in development.
Energy Transformation: Most power plants convert one form of energy to another. For example: Chemical energy (coal) → Heat energy → Kinetic energy (steam) → Electrical energy. The Law of Conservation of Energy states energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed.

Astronomy

Origins of the Universe

The Big Bang Theory

The Big Bang Theory is the prevailing cosmological model explaining the origin and evolution of the universe. It states that approximately 13.8 billion years ago, the universe began from an extremely hot, dense singular point (singularity) and has been expanding and cooling ever since.

1
Singularity (t=0) — All matter, energy, space, and time compressed into a single point of infinite density
2
Inflation (10⁻³⁶ – 10⁻³² seconds) — Universe expanded exponentially faster than light; temperature dropped dramatically
3
Quark Era — Quarks and gluons form; universe is a quark-gluon plasma soup
4
Hadron Era (1 second) — Quarks combine into protons and neutrons; matter-antimatter annihilation; slight matter surplus remains
5
Nucleosynthesis (3 minutes) — Protons and neutrons fuse into hydrogen and helium nuclei
6
Recombination (380,000 years) — Electrons combine with nuclei forming atoms; universe becomes transparent; Cosmic Microwave Background radiation released
7
First Stars & Galaxies (200–400 million years) — Gravity pulls matter into clumps; first stars ignite; heavy elements form in stellar cores

Evidence for the Big Bang

  • Hubble's Law (1929): Galaxies are moving away from us; the universe is expanding. Going backward in time implies everything was once together.
  • Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMB): Faint microwave radiation uniformly filling the universe — the "afterglow" of the Big Bang, discovered in 1965.
  • Abundance of Light Elements: The universe's composition (~75% hydrogen, ~25% helium) matches Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions.
  • Large-scale structure of the universe: The distribution of galaxies and galaxy clusters matches Big Bang predictions.

Dark Matter & Dark Energy

Dark Matter (~27%)Invisible matter that doesn't interact with light. Detected through gravitational effects on visible matter and galaxies. Its nature is unknown.
Dark Energy (~68%)Mysterious energy driving the accelerating expansion of the universe. Identified in 1998. Ordinary matter is only ~5% of universe.

Stars, Black Holes, and Galaxies

How Stars Form

Stars form in giant clouds of gas and dust called nebulae. Gravity causes regions within a nebula to collapse into a protostar. When core temperature reaches ~10 million°C, nuclear fusion ignites — the star is born. It enters the Main Sequence stage, where it spends most of its life fusing hydrogen into helium.

The Hertzsprung-Russell (H-R) Diagram

The H-R diagram plots stars by luminosity (brightness) vs. surface temperature. Most stars fall along the Main Sequence (a diagonal band). Giants and supergiants are above it; white dwarfs are below.

Stellar Life Cycles

Low/Medium Mass Stars (like Sun)Main Sequence → Red Giant → Planetary Nebula → White Dwarf → (eventually) Black Dwarf
High Mass StarsMain Sequence → Red Supergiant → Supernova → Neutron Star or Black Hole (if mass >3× Sun)

Supernovae

A supernova is an enormous stellar explosion — the most energetic event in the universe (briefly outshining entire galaxies). They distribute heavy elements (iron, gold, uranium) throughout space — elements heavier than iron can only be created in supernovae. The remnant may become a neutron star or black hole.

Black Holes

A black hole is a region where gravity is so extreme that nothing — not even light — can escape the event horizon (boundary of no return). Black holes form when massive stars (>20× solar mass) collapse. Key terms:

Event HorizonThe "point of no return" boundary. Once crossed, escape is impossible — even for light.
SingularityThe infinitely dense center of a black hole where known physics breaks down.
Schwarzschild RadiusThe radius at which an object's escape velocity equals the speed of light (defines event horizon size).
Hawking RadiationTheoretical radiation emitted near event horizon due to quantum effects; black holes slowly evaporate over eons.
Supermassive Black HolesFound at centers of most large galaxies. Milky Way's is Sagittarius A* (~4 million solar masses).

Galaxies

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, stellar remnants, gas, dust, and dark matter. Types:

SpiralDisk-shaped with spiral arms. Active star formation in arms. Example: Milky Way, Andromeda.
EllipticalOval to spherical. Older stars, little gas or dust, minimal star formation. Can be enormous.
IrregularNo defined shape. Often result of galaxy collisions. Example: Magellanic Clouds (Milky Way's satellite galaxies).
⭐ The Milky Way contains 100–400 billion stars, is about 100,000 light-years across, and our Solar System is located about 26,000 light-years from its center. The nearest large galaxy, Andromeda, is ~2.5 million light-years away and is on a collision course with the Milky Way (in ~4.5 billion years).

The Solar System

Our Solar System formed ~4.6 billion years ago from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust (the solar nebula). The Sun contains 99.86% of all the Solar System's mass.

The Sun

The Sun is a G-type main sequence star (~4.6 billion years old, halfway through its life). It fuses ~600 million tons of hydrogen into helium every second via nuclear fusion in its core, releasing energy as light and heat.

CoreTemperature ~15 million°C. Nuclear fusion occurs. Energy radiated outward.
Radiative ZoneEnergy moves as photons, bouncing for ~100,000 years to escape this layer.
Convective ZoneHot plasma rises, cools, sinks. Creates convection currents.
PhotosphereVisible surface. ~5,500°C. Sunspots (cooler, darker regions) appear here.
ChromosphereReddish layer above photosphere. Solar flares and prominences originate here.
CoronaOutermost layer. Extremely hot (~1–3 million°C). Visible during total solar eclipses.

Dwarf Planets & the Kuiper Belt

Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 by the IAU because it hasn't cleared its orbital neighborhood. Other dwarf planets include Eris, Ceres, Makemake, and Haumea. The Kuiper Belt is a disk of icy bodies beyond Neptune (~30–50 AU). The even more distant Oort Cloud is a spherical shell of icy objects that is the source of long-period comets.

Rotation and Revolution

RotationSpinning of a body on its own axis. Earth rotates once every 23 hours 56 minutes (a sidereal day). Causes day and night.
RevolutionOrbital motion of one body around another. Earth revolves around the Sun in 365.25 days (1 year). Causes the seasons and the year.

The Seasons

Seasons are caused by Earth's axial tilt of 23.5° — NOT by distance from the Sun. When the Northern Hemisphere is tilted toward the Sun, it experiences summer (more direct sunlight, longer days). When tilted away, it's winter.

EventDate (approx.)What Happens
Summer Solstice (N.H.)June 21Longest day, shortest night; Sun highest in sky; Northern Hemisphere tilted most toward Sun
Winter Solstice (N.H.)December 21Shortest day, longest night; Sun lowest in sky; Northern Hemisphere tilted most away from Sun
Vernal (Spring) EquinoxMarch 20Equal day and night (~12 hrs each); Sun directly over equator
Autumnal EquinoxSeptember 22Equal day and night; Sun directly over equator

Coriolis Effect

Earth's rotation causes moving objects (air, water) to be deflected to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere. This creates the curved paths of winds and ocean currents, and causes hurricanes and cyclones to spin in opposite directions in each hemisphere.

⭐ Perihelion and Aphelion: Earth is actually closest to the Sun (perihelion, ~147 million km) in early January, and farthest (aphelion, ~152 million km) in early July — proving that distance from the Sun does NOT cause our seasons.

Comets and Asteroids

Comets

Comets are icy, rocky bodies that orbit the Sun in elongated elliptical orbits. They originate from the Kuiper Belt (short-period comets, <200 year periods) or the Oort Cloud (long-period comets, thousands of years).

NucleusSolid core of ice, dust, rock, and frozen gases (CO₂, CH₄, NH₃). Size: a few km to tens of km.
ComaFuzzy atmosphere of gas and dust surrounding the nucleus as it approaches the Sun and ice sublimates.
Ion TailAlways points directly away from the Sun (solar wind pushes it). Bluish, made of ionized gas.
Dust TailCurved tail made of dust particles. Points away from Sun but follows orbit path slightly. Yellowish-white.

Famous Comets: Halley's Comet (appears every ~75 years; last seen 1986, next 2061), Hale-Bopp (1997 — one of the brightest of the 20th century), Comet NEOWISE (2020).

Meteor Showers

When Earth passes through a comet's debris trail, particles burn up in the atmosphere creating meteor showers. Famous showers include the Perseids (August, from Comet Swift-Tuttle), Leonids (November, from Comet Tempel-Tuttle), and Geminids (December).

Asteroids

Asteroids are rocky, metallic, or carbonaceous bodies orbiting the Sun, mostly found in the Asteroid Belt between Mars and Jupiter (~2.2–3.2 AU from Sun). They are leftover material from the solar system's formation that Jupiter's gravity prevented from forming a planet.

C-type (Carbonaceous)Most common (~75%). Dark, carbon-rich. Similar composition to the early solar nebula.
S-type (Silicaceous)~17% of asteroids. Made of silicate minerals and metal. Common in inner asteroid belt.
M-type (Metallic)Iron-nickel composition. May be cores of destroyed protoplanets. Target of mining missions.

Near-Earth Objects & Meteorites

Asteroids whose orbits bring them close to Earth are called Near-Earth Objects (NEOs). When a fragment enters Earth's atmosphere it's a meteor (shooting star). If it reaches the ground, it's a meteorite. The K-Pg extinction (65 million years ago, end of dinosaurs) is linked to a ~10 km asteroid impact at Chicxulub, Mexico.

Key Terms: Meteoroid = space rock in space | Meteor = burning up in atmosphere | Meteorite = reached Earth's surface

Phases of the Moon

The Moon's phases result from its position relative to Earth and the Sun as it orbits Earth. The Moon doesn't produce its own light — we see reflected sunlight. One complete cycle of phases takes ~29.5 days (a synodic month).

Tides

Tides are periodic rises and falls of sea levels caused by the gravitational pull of the Moon (primarily) and the Sun on Earth's oceans. The Moon pulls water toward it on the side facing the Moon and also on the opposite side due to inertia, creating two tidal bulges.

Spring TidesOccur at New Moon and Full Moon when Sun, Earth, and Moon align. Strongest tides — highest high tides, lowest low tides.
Neap TidesOccur at First and Last Quarter when Sun and Moon are at 90° to each other. Weakest tides — moderate highs and lows.

Synchronous Rotation (Tidal Locking)

The Moon rotates on its axis in exactly the same time it takes to orbit Earth (~27.3 days). This means we always see the same side of the Moon (the near side). This is called tidal locking and occurred over billions of years due to Earth's gravitational pull.

⭐ The Moon is moving away from Earth at about 3.8 cm per year due to tidal interactions. When the Moon formed ~4.5 billion years ago, it was much closer (resulting in much stronger tides and shorter days on Earth).

Solar and Lunar Eclipses

Eclipses occur when the Sun, Earth, and Moon align precisely. They don't happen every month because the Moon's orbit is tilted ~5° relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun.

Solar Eclipse

A solar eclipse occurs at New Moon when the Moon passes directly between the Sun and Earth, blocking sunlight from reaching parts of Earth.

Total Solar EclipseMoon completely covers the Sun. Only visible from within the Moon's umbra (darkest shadow). The Sun's corona becomes visible. Lasts up to ~7 minutes.
Partial Solar EclipseOnly part of the Sun is blocked by the Moon. Visible from the penumbra (partial shadow). Much larger area experiences this.
Annular Solar EclipseMoon is at apogee (farthest from Earth) and appears smaller. A ring ("annulus") of sunlight surrounds the Moon. "Ring of Fire."
Warning: Never look directly at the Sun during a solar eclipse without proper eclipse glasses (ISO 12312-2 certified). Even a small exposed sliver of the Sun can cause permanent eye damage (solar retinopathy).

Lunar Eclipse

A lunar eclipse occurs at Full Moon when Earth passes directly between the Sun and Moon, casting Earth's shadow onto the Moon. Visible from any location on Earth where the Moon is above the horizon.

Total Lunar EclipseMoon passes completely into Earth's umbra. Moon turns red/orange ("Blood Moon") — caused by sunlight refracted through Earth's atmosphere (same effect as sunsets).
Partial Lunar EclipseOnly part of the Moon enters Earth's umbra. Part of the Moon darkens noticeably.
Penumbral Lunar EclipseMoon passes through Earth's penumbra (outer shadow). Subtle darkening; often hard to notice. Most common type.

Why Eclipses Aren't Monthly

If the Moon's orbit were perfectly aligned with Earth's orbit around the Sun (the ecliptic), we would have both a solar and a lunar eclipse every month. However, the Moon's orbit is tilted ~5.1° to the ecliptic. Eclipses only occur when the Moon is at or near one of the two nodes (intersection points of the orbital planes) while also at New or Full Moon phase. These conditions align only about twice per year, resulting in eclipse seasons.

⭐ Saros Cycle: Eclipses repeat in a pattern every 18 years, 11 days, and 8 hours, known as the Saros cycle. Ancient astronomers used this to predict eclipses thousands of years ago before understanding why they occur.
